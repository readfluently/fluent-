Chapter 1

The Problem Begins

This chapter introduces the challenge. A developer is working productively, then suddenly notices that the laptop is slowing down.

Most people think building a software application is about writing code. They imagine developers typing quickly, solving problems with ease. What they do not always see are the difficult moments when things stop working and the real challenge becomes the person behind the screen.

This is a story about one of those moments. It started as a normal working session. A developer was building an educational application called Fluent, a platform designed to help learners improve their reading skills using modern AI tools.

Several tools were open at the same time: a code editor, a browser, AI assistants, and a word processor for planning content. At first, everything felt productive. Then the laptop started to slow down.

The signs were small at first. Typed letters appeared slowly. Clicks took longer to respond. Then the desktop background suddenly turned black, although the icons were still there. The system looked like it was still working, but it was struggling.

“My first thought was simple: What did I break?”

Chapter 2

Thinking Like a Debugger

This chapter shows how the developer begins to investigate the problem carefully instead of panicking.

When working with technology, people often assume the problem must be something they have just done. The developer remembered there were many pending commits in the project and wondered whether that had caused the issue.

But instead of panicking, the developer started thinking like a debugger. Real debugging is more than fixing code errors. It involves asking: What changed? What stayed the same? What assumptions am I making? What evidence do I actually have?

The system memory was checked. The laptop had 8GB of RAM, and about 85% of it was in use. That was high. Yet something still did not make sense. By the time this was checked, several programs had already been closed. Why was the memory still so full?

This led to an important lesson: sometimes the problem you see is not exactly the problem that exists. Systems can take time to recover. The cause may be gone, but the effects can remain for a while.

Chapter 3

Finding the Real Cause

This chapter explains how the developer narrows down the problem and discovers that the laptop is overwhelmed, not broken.

The developer began eliminating possible causes one by one. The Git commits were not the issue. Commits are simply records of changes and do not usually consume large amounts of system resources. The processor was not the issue either, because CPU usage was only around 30%.

That meant the computer was not struggling to think — it was struggling to remember.

Eventually the answer became clear. It was not one large mistake. It was many small demands happening at the same time. The browser, development tools, AI assistants, and background processes were all competing for limited memory.

The laptop was not broken. It was overwhelmed.

This created another important understanding: building software is not only about making programs work. It is also about learning how to manage limited resources — time, attention, memory, and energy.

💻 Developer Insight

Many technical problems are not caused by dramatic failures. Sometimes they happen because several ordinary tasks are all demanding attention at once. Good debugging often begins by testing simple explanations first.

Chapter 4

Debugging as a Life Skill

This final chapter connects the laptop problem to growth mindset, persistence, learning, and everyday problem solving.

Good developers are not the ones who never encounter problems. They are the ones who learn how to respond when problems appear. They investigate instead of panicking. They test ideas instead of guessing wildly. They understand that confusion is often part of learning.

This is called a mindset of growth. A growth mindset means understanding that difficulty is not a sign that you cannot do something. It may simply mean you are doing something challenging enough to help you grow.

In that moment, the developer could have become frustrated and assumed a lack of skill or experience. Instead, the problem became a learning opportunity. There was a lesson about managing tools, restarting when necessary, and recognising resource limits instead of treating every problem as a personal failure.

The story then expands beyond technology. In school, when something goes wrong, a learner may think, “Maybe I am not good at this.” But debugging thinking asks different questions:

“Did I prepare properly? Am I using the right strategy? Am I trying to do too many things at once? What small change can I test?”

Later, after restarting the laptop and organising the workflow, everything ran smoothly again. But the most important improvement was not in the computer. It was in the developer’s thinking.

Struggling does not always mean failing. Often, struggling means you are building something difficult — and difficult things are worth building. That is why persistence matters.
